Abstract

In the era of digital communication, deepfake technology poses a critical threat to the integrity of information disseminated online. These AI-generated videos can realistically depict individuals saying or doing things they never did, with significant implications for public discourse, human rights, and digital media authenticity.

This project develops a robust detection system using Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) to identify manipulated videos by analyzing pixel-level discrepancies and temporal anomalies across video frames. The model is trained on over 10,000 authentic and deepfake videos and achieves real-time detection at 24 FPS on standard hardware.

Interview Q&A

Q1: What motivated you to choose deepfake detection as your project?
Deepfakes are a major and rapidly growing threat to online trust and security. I wanted to work on a solution that combines AI and real-world impact, helping people and organizations verify digital content and fight misinformation.
Q2: How does your deepfake detection system work?
The system analyzes video frames using a GRU-based AI model. GRUs capture subtle sequence changes and pixel anomalies that indicate fake content. We preprocess the video, extract 16 evenly-spaced frames, and the model predicts whether the video is genuine or manipulated with a confidence score.
Q3: Why did you use GRU instead of other neural networks?
GRUs are well-suited for time-series and frame-sequence analysis, making them ideal for video content. They use fewer resources than LSTMs, so the model is fast and practical for real-time detection — crucial for production deployment.
Q4: What challenges did you face and how did you overcome them?
The biggest challenges were managing large video datasets and tuning the model to minimize false positives. We addressed this by optimizing frame selection strategies, applying data augmentation, and carefully tuning GRU hyperparameters — achieving 25% fewer false positives than baseline CNN methods.
Q5: What are the future improvements you plan?
The project can be expanded to cover deepfake audio detection and integrated into social media platforms for live content verification. We also plan to explore transformer-based architectures and build a real-time streaming pipeline for platforms like YouTube or Amazon Video.

My Role in the Project

I was actively involved across all stages: data collection and labeling of real and deepfake video datasets, video preprocessing using OpenCV (frame extraction, resizing, normalization), designing and training the CNN/GRU model using TensorFlow/Keras, evaluating performance using accuracy and loss metrics, and integrating prediction results with a MySQL database backend.
